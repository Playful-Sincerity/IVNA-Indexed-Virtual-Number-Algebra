# Books on Writing Exceptional Research Papers
## Research Findings — 2026-03-31

This document catalogs books and resources on writing mathematically rigorous, philosophically compelling, and paradigm-shifting research papers. Focused on: mathematical writing craft, philosophy of discovery, academic persuasion, and presenting novel frameworks to skeptical audiences.

---

## Category A: Mathematical Writing Craft

### 1. "How to Write Mathematics" — Paul R. Halmos (1970)
**Published in:** L'Enseignement Mathematique, Vol. 16  
**Free PDF sources:**
- https://www.math.lsu.edu/~oxley/halmos.pdf
- https://sites.stat.columbia.edu/liam/teaching/HalmosWrite.pdf
- https://entropiesschool.sciencesconf.org/data/How_to_Write_Mathematics.pdf

**Why it matters:** The canonical essay on mathematical exposition. Halmos covers everything from content to audience to organization to fine points of language and symbolism. The most cited guide in the mathematical community.

**Key principles extracted:**
1. Have something to say — "To have something to say is by far the most important ingredient of good exposition." Don't say nothing, don't say everything.
2. Write for someone specific — Identify your audience in both a general sense (e.g., second-year graduate students) and a specific, personal sense (imagine a particular person you've discussed the subject with).
3. Organize — "Arrange the material so as to minimize the resistance and maximize the insight of the reader." Create an outline before writing anything.
4. Choose notation carefully — Avoid confusing symbols; don't use a letter for two different purposes; design the notation system before writing.
5. Write in spirals — Draft chapters multiple times in increasing depth. Early versions are rough; later passes refine. This O(n^2) algorithm prevents local over-polish at the expense of global coherence.
6. Use "we" correctly — The editorial "we" means "writer and reader together," not the royal "we." It invites the reader into the journey.
7. Minimize symbols — Prefer words to symbols wherever natural English works equally well. A sentence should be grammatically complete.
8. Never begin a sentence with a symbol — Write "The function f..." not "f is..."
9. Be honest about "obvious" — Question every use of "clearly" or "obviously." If it requires thought, show the thought.
10. Stop when done — Do not pad after making your point.

**Full AMS Collection:** "How to Write Mathematics" (1973) — Steenrod, Halmos, Schiffer, Dieudonne  
Available at: https://bookstore.ams.org/HWM  
This four-essay collection covers: formal vs. informal structure (Steenrod), writing craft (Halmos), types of expository writing (Schiffer), and overview (Dieudonne). Steenrod's distinction between "formal structure" (definitions, theorems, proofs) and "informal structure" (motivations, analogies, examples) is particularly useful — he argues this division must be conspicuously maintained because the logical structure must be clear.

### 2. "Mathematical Writing" — Knuth, Larrabee, Roberts (1989)
**Publisher:** Mathematical Association of America (MAA Notes, No. 14)  
**Free PDF sources:**
- https://jmlr.csail.mit.edu/reviewing-papers/knuth_mathematical_writing.pdf
- https://www-cs-faculty.stanford.edu/~knuth/papers/cs1193.pdf (Stanford official report)

**Why it matters:** Transcript of Knuth's 1987 Stanford course on mathematical writing, featuring guest lectures from Paul Halmos, Leslie Lamport, Herb Wilf, and others. Covers everything from prose style to algorithm documentation to the process of refereeing. One of the most comprehensive and concrete guides available.

**Key principles extracted:**
- Avoid excessive subscripts and superscripts — they create visual noise and slow reading
- Use "we" to mean "author and reader together" — it creates collaborative engagement
- The primary goal is to "bring the reader on a rewarding mathematical journey" (Poonen, building on Knuth)
- Learn to create effective diagrams and tables — visual communication is part of mathematical exposition
- Study the literature in your field before writing — you must know conventions to depart from them consciously
- Documentation of algorithms requires the same care as proofs — each step must be justified

### 3. "A Primer of Mathematical Writing" — Steven G. Krantz (2nd ed., 2017)
**Publisher:** American Mathematical Society  
**arXiv preprint:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1612.04888

**Why it matters:** Practical, comprehensive guide covering grammar, syntax, usage, TeX typesetting, email etiquette, publishing, refereeing, and all aspects of academic mathematical life. Good for concrete technical advice.

**Key principles extracted:**
- Don't agonize over each word while writing a first draft — get ideas on the page; agonize during editing
- Never use "very" unless you'd be comfortable using "damn" in its place (tests whether intensification is warranted)
- Don't use the same word, or even the same sound, in two consecutive sentences — creates unintentional rhythm
- A good mathematical sentence is complete, with subject, verb, and object
- Define terms at first use; never assume the reader shares your notation conventions

### 4. "Handbook of Writing for the Mathematical Sciences" — Nicholas J. Higham (3rd ed., 2020)
**Publisher:** SIAM  
**Author's page:** https://nhigham.com/handbook-of-writing-for-the-mathematical-sciences/

**Why it matters:** Comprehensive modern handbook covering everything from general writing principles through mathematical typesetting, publishing, giving talks, and writing theses. Incorporates modern digital publishing. A full-spectrum professional reference.

**Topics covered (from table of contents):**
- General principles of scientific writing
- Mathematical writing (notation, equations, symbols vs. words)
- English usage and grammar
- Writing when English is a foreign language
- Writing a paper from scratch
- Revising a draft
- Publishing a paper
- Writing and defending a thesis
- Writing a talk / giving a talk / preparing a poster
- TeX and LaTeX

### 5. "Practical Suggestions for Mathematical Writing" — Bjorn Poonen (MIT)
**Free PDF:** https://math.mit.edu/~poonen/papers/writing.pdf  
**Also in:** AMS Notices, June 2021

**Why it matters:** Concise, densely practical guide from a leading number theorist. Excellent on sentence-level clarity and proof structure.

**Key principles extracted:**
- "The primary goal of an article is to bring the reader on a rewarding mathematical journey." The writer is a guide, not a gatekeeper.
- A reader reaching the period of a sentence should know why each claim up to that point is true
- If a claim does not follow from the previous sentence alone, say what it does follow from
- In a chain of equalities, make clear which reason justifies which equality
- Omit filler phrases like "We remark that" and "Note that" — except when needed to prevent a sentence from starting with a symbol
- Two sentences can be connected by a semicolon; not just a comma
- Adverbs like "therefore" and "hence" do not join sentences — conjunctions like "and," "but," and "so" do (with a preceding comma)

### 6. "How to Write Mathematical Papers" — Bruce C. Berndt
**Free PDF:** https://math.illinois.edu/sites/default/files/2025-04/Berndt_writingmath.pdf

**Key principles extracted:**
- The title should have no more than ten words and be informative — "On a conjecture of Daisy Dud" is useless unless everyone knows who she is
- The Introduction is the most important part of your paper — Berndt advocates writing it first to organize thinking
- Get to the purpose of the paper as soon as possible; don't begin with notation piles
- Every non-trivial step should be documented and steps should be related in sentences
- Display complicated expressions rather than embedding them in text
- "Your paper should be attractive, neat, and beautiful — this puts editors and referees in a good mood"

---

### 7. "How to Write a Clear Math Paper: Some 21st Century Tips" — Igor Pak (UCLA)
**Free PDF:** https://www.math.ucla.edu/~pak/papers/how-to-write1.pdf

**Key principles extracted:**
- Clarity is the foundational principle — "For the sake of clarity, ignore all rules"
- Include a table of contents for longer papers
- Understand why citations matter, not just how to format them — citations situate your work in context
- "Downshift your style" — write at one level below your natural register to increase accessibility
- Avoid being pedantic or pretentious — it undermines the mathematics
- Read quality nonfiction writing guides before starting — mathematical writing skills transfer from general writing
- Recognize that writing is never truly finished — revise substantially and republish when appropriate
- Advertise and popularize your work after publication

### 8. "Writing a Research Paper in Mathematics" — J. Rickert (MIT)
**Free web page:** https://web.mit.edu/jrickert/www/mathadvice.html

**Key principles extracted:**
- "Written communication acts as a filter through which your mathematical work is viewed"
- Before writing, ask: Does your result strengthen previous work? Prove equivalence? Connect unrelated fields? Apply new methods to old problems? Classify structures? Resolve special cases? — the answer shapes the framing
- Formal structure = definitions, theorems, proofs; Informal structure = motivations, analogies, examples, explanations — both are required
- Develop logical outline before writing prose
- Name results appropriately: lemmas as foundations, theorems as substance, corollaries as extensions
- Extract common lemmas — if two proofs repeat techniques, there's an underlying general lemma
- Write proofs forward — begin with hypotheses and proceed to conclusion, do not reverse-engineer
- Avoid "any" — replace with "each" or "every" to remove ambiguity
- Avoid "where" clauses — they indicate a lazy afterthought; restructure the sentence
- Do not use "equivalent" for theorems unless you mean logical equivalence

### 9. "How to Write a Proof" — Leslie Lamport (1993)
**Free PDF:** https://lamport.azurewebsites.net/pubs/lamport-how-to-write.pdf

**Why it matters:** Lamport proposes hierarchically structured proofs that make each logical step explicit. His discovery: when colleagues wrote proofs in structured style, almost every one had serious mistakes — even when the theorems were correct. Conventional proofs hide errors.

**Key principles extracted:**
- Structured proofs force each step to be explicitly stated, making it hard to skip justified steps
- The conventional proof style makes it easy to assert "this case is similar" when it isn't
- When asked to write structured proofs, conjectures that seemed easily provable were often found to be false
- Skeptical readers who check proofs prefer structured proofs because errors become obvious

### 10. "How to Write Mathematics Badly" — Jean-Pierre Serre (2003)
**Transcript (ETH Zurich):** https://metaphor.ethz.ch/x/2025/hs/401-3005-00L/texts/serre_how_to_write_mathematics_badly_transcript.pdf  
**Video:** Available on YouTube (56-minute Harvard talk)  
**Blog discussion:** https://golem.ph.utexas.edu/category/2007/02/how_to_write_mathematics_badly.html

**Why it matters:** Serre (Fields Medal, Abel Prize) gives an ironic masterclass by demonstrating what not to do. Extremely specific and practical. Serre was part of Bourbaki and is considered one of the finest mathematical writers.

**Key principles extracted:**
- Never make implicit identifications between structures — always provide explicit morphisms
- Name all morphisms; give maps clear names with specified properties
- Use "a" and "the" precisely — "a function" means any function satisfying conditions; "the function" means a canonical specific one
- Never start a sentence with a symbol — it creates parsing ambiguity
- Mind basic English grammar — "its" vs. "it's," comma usage, subject-verb agreement
- Define any term that has multiple definitions in the literature — "twist," for example, means different things in different fields
- Mathematical writing is like language: "Some mathematicians have a good ear; some not"

## Category B: Philosophy of Mathematics and Scientific Discovery

### 11. "Proofs and Refutations" — Imre Lakatos (1976)
**Publisher:** Cambridge University Press  
**Internet Archive (restricted borrow):** https://archive.org/details/proofsrefutation0000laka  
**Partial free text (Cornell):** https://e.math.cornell.edu/people/mann/classes/chicago/Lakatos.pdf

**Why it matters:** The most important philosophical work for anyone presenting a genuinely novel mathematical framework. Lakatos shows that mathematics advances not by accumulation of established truths but through a dynamic process of conjectures, proofs, and counterexamples. Definitions evolve. What counts as a "proof" changes. This is both liberating (your framework doesn't need to be final) and strategic (anticipate counterexamples; incorporate them).

**Key ideas:**
- Mathematics grows through "proofs and refutations" — primitive conjecture, rough proof, counterexample, refinement, new conjecture
- Definitions are not fixed — they must be "patched up in the light of later insights, in particular failed proofs"
- Counterexamples are not failures — they are opportunities to sharpen definitions and discover deeper structure
- The process: primitive conjecture → rough proof-sketch → global counterexample → locate the "guilty lemma" → refine conjecture → new field opens
- This gives mathematics "a somewhat experimental flavour" — it is more like science than its formal presentation suggests
- Lakatos challenges formalism: informal mathematics is fallible, dynamic, and rational — not the sterile accumulation of certified truths
- For presenting a new framework: expect your critics to find "monster-barring" counterexamples; have refined definitions ready; show how your framework incorporates the most challenging cases

**Full structure of Lakatos's method:**
1. Primitive conjecture
2. Proof — a rough thought-experiment decomposing the conjecture into subconjectures
3. Global counterexamples emerge
4. The "guilty lemma" is located — the step to which the counterexample is local
5. Previously accepted consequences are checked
6. Counterexamples become new examples — new fields of inquiry open

### 12. "The Structure of Scientific Revolutions" — Thomas S. Kuhn (1962)
**Internet Archive (full text, PDF, EPUB):** https://archive.org/details/thomas-s.-kuhn-the-structure-of-scientific-revolutions  
**Note:** The 1990 scan on Archive.org is in public domain format with PDF/EPUB download available

**Why it matters:** The foundational text for understanding how paradigm shifts happen and why they are so difficult. Essential for anyone presenting work that challenges existing frameworks.

**Key ideas:**
- Normal science operates within a "paradigm" — a shared framework of assumptions, exemplary problems, and standards
- Anomalies accumulate: puzzles that normal science cannot solve
- Crisis emerges when anomalies cannot be ignored or explained away
- Revolution: a new paradigm that resolves the anomalies — but this involves a gestalt shift, not just adding new facts
- "Paradigm shifts aren't logical debates but involve community-wide perspective changes"
- After a revolution, the old and new paradigms are "incommensurable" — they literally see the world differently
- **For presenting a new framework:** Document the anomalies in the current paradigm; show your framework resolves them; expect resistance from those heavily invested in the existing paradigm; find early adopters rather than trying to convert true believers

**Chapters:**
1. Introduction: A Role for History
2. The Route to Normal Science
3. The Nature of Normal Science
4. Normal Science as Puzzle-solving
5. The Priority of Paradigms
6. Anomaly and the Emergence of Scientific Discoveries
7. Crisis and the Emergence of Scientific Theories
8. The Response to Crisis
9. The Nature and Necessity of Scientific Revolutions
10. Revolutions as Changes of World View
11. The Invisibility of Revolutions
12. The Resolutions of Revolutions
13. Progress through Revolutions
14. Postscript—1969

### 13. "How to Solve It" — George Polya (1945)
**Internet Archive (full text):** https://archive.org/details/polya-how-to-solve-it

**Why it matters:** The foundational text on mathematical heuristics — the art of guided discovery. Polya's four-step method is relevant not just to problem-solving but to mathematical presentation: a paper that mirrors the logic of discovery is more persuasive than one that presents a finished proof without context.

**Key principles:**
- Four-step method: Understand the problem → Devise a plan → Carry out the plan → Look back
- Heuristic strategies: analogy, specialization, generalization, working backwards, auxiliary elements
- Mathematical thinking involves "plausible reasoning" alongside proof — intuition precedes rigor
- Good exposition mirrors the process of discovery, not just the final polished result
- Ask: "What is the unknown? What are the data? What is the condition?" — these questions structure both problem-solving and mathematical writing

### 14. "A Mathematician's Apology" — G.H. Hardy (1940)
**Free PDF:** https://www.arvindguptatoys.com/arvindgupta/mathsapology-hardy.pdf

**Why it matters:** The canonical defense of mathematical beauty as intrinsic value. Hardy's aesthetic criteria for mathematical work remain the most cited in the literature.

**Key ideas:**
- "A mathematician, like a painter or poet, is a maker of patterns. The mathematician's patterns must be beautiful; the ideas must fit together in a harmonious way."
- Beauty in mathematics involves: unexpectedness, inevitability, and economy
- These three aesthetic criteria apply to papers as well: the result should surprise, the proof should feel inevitable in retrospect, and nothing should be extraneous
- "Exposition, criticism, appreciation, is work for second-rate minds" — Hardy was wrong about this, but understanding his view clarifies why mathematical writing is undervalued and what must overcome that bias
- Pure mathematics prizes: significance (not triviality), generality, depth, unexpectedness, inevitability, economy

## Category C: Academic Writing and Persuasion

### 15. "The Sense of Style" — Steven Pinker (2014)
**Publisher:** Viking/Penguin  
**Summary:** https://www.themarginalian.org/2014/10/10/steven-pinker-sense-of-style/

**Why it matters:** A cognitive scientist's analysis of why academic writing fails and what makes writing genuinely clear. Applies linguistics and psychology to writing instruction. Particularly useful for understanding why even highly intelligent writers produce unreadable prose.

**Key principles:**
- Classic style: the writer shows the reader something in the world. Writing is a transparent window, not a display of erudition.
- "The curse of knowledge" — the most common cause of bad writing: writers cannot remember what it was like not to know what they now know, so they omit essential context
- Writing is an act of "imagining a reader" — you must consciously construct a model of who you are writing for
- Good writers are avid readers — they have internalized examples and can reverse-engineer effective techniques
- Clarity, trust, beauty — three reasons style matters
- Avoid "hedgehog" academic prose (elaborate hedging, passive voice, jargon walls) in favor of direct assertion backed by evidence
- Rules exist for reasons — understand the reason to know when to break the rule

---

### 16. "Stylish Academic Writing" — Helen Sword (2012)
**Publisher:** Harvard University Press

**Why it matters:** Sword analyzed over 1,000 peer-reviewed articles and found a startling gap between what academics say good writing looks like and what they actually produce. Based on empirical research.

**Key principles:**
- "Elegant ideas deserve elegant expression"
- Six universally agreed elements of stylish writing: clarity, coherence, concision; short or mixed-length sentences; plain English; precision; active verbs; telling a story
- Use anecdotes and carefully chosen metaphors — they are not unscholarly, they are effective
- Opening sentences should make readers want to continue
- The myth that complex ideas require complex prose is empirically false — the most cited papers are often the most clearly written
- "Jargon walls" are professional insecurity, not precision

---

### 17. "Economical Writing" — Deirdre McCloskey (3rd ed.)
**Publisher:** University of Chicago Press  
**Key principles summary:** https://www.paragraphai.com/post/economical-writing-book-rules-by-deirdre-mccloskey

**Why it matters:** 35 pithy rules for writing clear, persuasive academic prose. McCloskey is an economist who writes about writing — her advice is battle-tested in one of the most jargon-dense fields.

**Key principles:**
- If you write to impress, it will fail — write to communicate
- The best sentence contains no unnecessary words (builds on Strunk & White)
- Use active voice and strong verbs
- Revision is the real work of writing — economical writing results from careful editing, not natural flow
- Six organizing principles: clarity, brevity, reader engagement, logical structure, revision, integration
- Write with an eye toward what the reader will take away, not what you want to display

---

### 18. "The Elements of Style" — Strunk & White (4th ed.)
**Free PDF:** https://ia801500.us.archive.org/26/items/pdfy-2_qp8jQ61OI6NHwa/Strunk%20%26%20White%20-%20The%20Elements%20of%20Style,%204th%20Edition.pdf

**Key principles:**
- Omit needless words — "Vigorous writing is concise... every word should tell"
- Use active voice
- Put statements in positive form — say what is, not what isn't
- Choose a suitable design and stick to it
- Make the paragraph the unit of composition
- Use definite, specific, concrete language
- Express coordinate ideas in similar form (parallelism)
- Place the emphatic words at the end of a sentence

## Category D: Ten Rules for Structuring Papers

### 19. "Ten Simple Rules for Structuring Papers" — Mensh & Kording (2017)
**Free full text:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5619685/

**Why it matters:** Empirically grounded rules for structuring papers to maximize persuasiveness and impact. Context-Content-Conclusion structure at every scale.

**Ten rules:**
1. Focus on one central contribution — papers trying to make multiple points are less convincing about each
2. Write for naive readers — define terms, minimize cognitive load
3. Use Context-Content-Conclusion (C-C-C) structure at every level
4. Optimize logical flow — present related ideas consecutively; use parallel structure
5. Tell a complete story in the abstract — C-C-C structure; start broad, narrow to the gap, broaden to significance
6. Communicate why the paper matters in the introduction — establish a gap progressively
7. Present results as logically connected statements — figures communicate conclusions
8. Discuss limitations and field relevance in the discussion
9. Allocate time strategically — titles, abstracts, and figures receive far more attention than methods
10. Iterate through feedback — treat writing as optimization requiring multiple reviewers

## Category E: Presenting Novel Frameworks to Skeptical Audiences

### 20. Lessons from History of Ignored Discoveries (Nintil research)
**Source:** https://nintil.com/discoveries-ignored/

**Patterns of rejection:**
- Outsider status (from non-prestigious institution) triggers automatic skepticism
- Noisy data makes replication difficult, creating lasting doubt
- Paradigm conflicts: ideas contradicting existing models are dismissed without examination
- Wrong mechanism, right conclusion: correct finding through flawed reasoning gets rejected entirely

**Practical strategies for new paradigm advocates:**
- Expect 5-15 journal rejections — mRNA vaccines, CRISPR, PCR all faced this
- Build diverse funding sources — don't rely on a single grant source
- Recruit established allies within the field — one established supporter is worth dozens of sympathetic strangers
- Frame work conservatively at first, expand implications after initial acceptance
- Replicate findings rigorously — overcome historical skepticism through reproducibility
- Accept partial credit rather than claiming to revolutionize everything
- Document early rejection — it becomes validation after success

### 21. Resistance to Revolutionary Theories (ScienceDirect)
**Pattern from research on revolutionary theory acceptance:**
- Three stages of resistance: "not true" → "true but unimportant" → "true, important, but we knew it all along"
- A new theory needs evidence AND explanation — neither alone is sufficient
- Great ideas sometimes arrive too early — they need new data, technology, or infrastructure to be realized
- The scientific community appropriately contains both bold and conservative individuals — this is healthy, not hostile

## Category F: Advice from Master Mathematical Writers

### 22. Terence Tao's Writing Advice Blog
**Main page:** https://terrytao.wordpress.com/advice-on-writing-papers/

**Key principles:**
- Use "liberal amounts of plain English" — English communicates on multiple levels beyond what symbols can
- Use connectors purposefully: "also/furthermore" (equal importance), "therefore/since" (logical consequence), "however/but" (contrast), "in particular" (specificity), "more generally" (broader scope), "similarly" (analogy without deduction)
- Reserve notation for "standard formal computations" where readers already understand the general approach
- "The primary purpose of mathematical writing is to communicate and inform, not to impress"
- Develop your own individual style rather than mimicking the literature — this preserves intellectual honesty about which insights are yours
- Write professionally while maintaining your authentic voice

### 23. Quintilian's Principle (via Halmos)
"We should not write so that it is possible for our readers to understand us, but so that it is impossible for them to misunderstand us."

This 2,000-year-old standard — cited in Halmos discussions — is the highest bar for mathematical exposition.
